Revised:

Nowadays, in the current knowledge-based society, the accessibility of a university education holds significant importance in securing good employment opportunities. Some individuals argue that higher education should be made universally accessible and that governments should provide free education to all students, regardless of their financial background. In my opinion, while there are several advantages to this proposal, the drawbacks outweigh the benefits.

It is widely acknowledged that providing tertiary education without any tuition fees can have a number of positive implications for individuals and society as a whole. This is because it allows more people to pursue specialized knowledge, thereby increasing their chances of obtaining well-paying jobs. Additionally, it contributes to the production of a more educated workforce, which ultimately benefits the country's economy. Furthermore, making education free can level the playing field for all students, particularly those from low-income backgrounds who would otherwise be unable to afford university. Consequently, these changes can lead to economic improvements and reduce the wealth gap in society.

However, this perspective is flawed due to the long-term negative consequences it could have on a country's overall development. Firstly, if everyone is entitled to free university education, it could reduce competition among students, leading to decreased motivation to enhance their academic abilities. Moreover, free education at the tertiary level may result in a lack of incentive for students to work hard, potentially undermining the value and quality of education in universities. This, in turn, could impact the proficiency of the workforce, which is crucial for a country's progress. Additionally, allocating a significant portion of the budget for free education might divert funds from other important sectors such as transportation, economy, and healthcare, potentially leading to increased taxes and reduced social welfare programs. Consequently, the standard of living for residents may be negatively affected.

In conclusion, I strongly believe that governments should not implement universal free tertiary education due to the potential risks associated with it. While it is true that offering free education can bring certain benefits, such as improved job prospects and reduced inequality, the negative consequences, including reduced student motivation and compromised education quality, outweigh the advantages. Therefore, it is crucial for governments to carefully consider the potential repercussions before implementing such policies.
