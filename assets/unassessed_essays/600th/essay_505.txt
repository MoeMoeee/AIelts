Revised:

In today's world, obesity is becoming a significant problem in many parts of the world. Recently, the number of obese people has been increasing rapidly, and some suggest that the main culprits, such as fast food and unhealthy food, should be taxed to discourage people from consuming too much. This essay will argue that increasing the price of junk food will not effectively help people fight this obesity crisis.

Obesity is a relatively modern problem that has never been so widespread in human history. In the past, it was rare to find overweight individuals among low-income households. However, in recent times, a significant portion of the obese population comes from these households. This is primarily due to the fact that low-income individuals can typically only afford low-quality and low-priced fast food or heavily processed food. These options have low nutritional value but contain high amounts of sugar and fat to compensate for the lower quality ingredients used to keep costs low. Levying taxes on the junk food industry will exacerbate the problem, as those who can barely afford to consume low-quality products will be forced to seek even lower-quality alternatives.

Instead of implementing a tax on low-quality food, it would be more effective to allocate funds to subsidize or reduce taxes on healthy food options. If overconsumption of low-quality products is the root cause of obesity, then the solution lies in changing consumer choices. It is impractical to expect individuals, especially those from low-income backgrounds, to simply eat less when they are already struggling financially. By providing incentives, such as lower taxes on healthier options or subsidies on high-quality unprocessed ingredients, to fast food chains and food manufacturers, healthier products can be made more accessible and affordable. This approach will be particularly beneficial for the lower class, who are most vulnerable to the obesity crisis.

In conclusion, obesity often stems from a lack of choices rather than personal preferences. For those individuals who have limited options and rely on fattening foods for sustenance, providing healthier and affordable alternatives would be far more effective than simply increasing the prices of junk food, which remains their only viable option. Therefore, I strongly disagree with the notion of increasing fast food prices as a solution to the obesity problem.
