Essay:

"It is undeniable that education plays an important role in people's lives , so according to some, education ought to be free of cost for society and some money should be allotted by the government to education and its management should be done by the state. In my opinion, offering everyone free education by the government would be problematic due to the heavy pressure on the government's shoulders and its bad effects on the efforts of students.

One of the reasons is that abolishing educational fees at all levels would put a tremendous burden on the suffering economy of most  countries around the world, especially developing countries. This means that developing countries have been struggling with fundamental issues, which are more crucial than literacy, and thus allocating some heavy financial aid to educational facilities would reduce the money for tackling  more essential problems including poverty, unemployment and the high rate of infectious diseases. For example, providing free education for people who are suffering from dangerous illnesses in underprivileged regions would not be advantageous for them.

Another important factor to consider is that eliminating educational fees in all grades might decrease the motivation of students. In other words, because of providing free of cost education regardless of students' scores, students would not be incentivised to work hard for better scores or take their studies seriously. Since free elementary, secondary and higher education are guaranteed for all pupils by the state, they are not motivated to put their whole heart into learning their lessons and be the best student for getting scholarships. For instance, the majority of young adults are trying to study hard and get full marks in university exams to be funded by famous British universities.

In conclusion, managing and providing educational materials and facilities freely would be problematic due to its financial aspects for most regions and decreasing level of motivation among students."


Revised:

"It is widely believed that education is essential for individuals and societies. Some argue that education should be accessible to all citizens without any cost, and the government should shoulder the responsibility of funding and managing it. However, I partially disagree with this viewpoint. While providing free education may seem ideal, it can create financial burdens for the government and diminish students' motivation.

Firstly, completely abolishing educational fees would impose an immense strain on the economy, particularly in developing countries. These nations already face pressing issues such as poverty, unemployment, and infectious diseases. Redirecting a significant amount of financial aid to education could hinder efforts to address these fundamental problems. For instance, prioritizing free education for individuals suffering from life-threatening illnesses would be more beneficial than offering it universally.

In addition, making education free at all levels might diminish students' incentive to work hard. When education is readily available to all, regardless of academic achievements, students may lack the motivation to strive for excellence or take their studies seriously. In countries where elementary, secondary, and higher education are guaranteed by the state, students may not be driven to excel in their studies in order to secure scholarships or opportunities for further advancement. For example, many young adults strive diligently to obtain top marks in university entrance exams to secure funding from prestigious British universities.

To conclude, while the idea of government-funded and managed education for all may seem appealing, it is not without its drawbacks. The financial strain it places on economies, particularly in developing countries, and the potential decrease in student motivation are significant concerns. Therefore, a balanced approach, such as providing subsidized education based on academic achievements or financial need, may be more viable. This would ensure educational opportunities are accessible while encouraging students to strive for excellence."
