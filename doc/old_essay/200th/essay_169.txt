Topic:

"An increasing number of professionals such as doctors and  teachers are leaving their own poorer countries to work in developed countries. What problem does this cause? What can be done to deal with this situation?"

Essay:

"Nowadays, the job scale in the developing countries is decreasing progressively. However, the economic growth of the rapidly growing countries is increasing by increased number of migrations of the professionals from the backward world countries. Following essay will look at the core reasons and propose some solid solutions.
One of the main causes of the complication is the lack of practical task in education systems. Their course is not updated. This would result in the difference between the academic life and performance of the students. For example, environmental engineers are required to develop the new models for minimizing waste. The course at their universities is theoretically based. And when they start their jobs, they enable to use the knowledge in the execution of the model. The solution is that the educational institutions should upgrade their course according to market demand.
Another trouble is that the doctors and teachers are highly paid in the developed countries. It can be said that job opportunities in the countries like Pakistan are fewer. Contrarily, there are many ways of growth in the countries like US and Australia. To tackle such issue, the Board of Directors must come to the front line and take steps to revise the salaries of the specialists in order to reduce relocation from their countries.
A third cause of the problem is the work hours. The jobs in the poor countries are nine to five per day. This would end up in minimum socializing, and it is hard for the people to find time even for themselves. In contrast, job timing is specified by the Administration, in Europe. They are allowed to carry out their profession in 6 to 7 hours a day. The issue is only resolved if Management allocates special timings for all types of labour.
To sum up, migration of the skilled workers will lead to skill shortage and poorer economic growth due to lack of product placements after graduation. That is a serious dilemma, and unless Government launches new schemes for employments with a handsome pay."


Revised:

In recent years, there has been a growing trend of professionals such as doctors and teachers leaving their poorer countries to work in developed nations. This phenomenon has resulted in several problems, and in this essay, I will discuss these issues and suggest potential solutions.

One significant problem of this trend is the lack of practical education in developing nations. The education system does not update its curricula, resulting in a gap between academic knowledge and practical skills. For example, while studying environmental engineering, students may learn theoretical models for reducing waste, but lack the hands-on experience to implement such strategies professionally. The solution to this problem would be for educational institutions to upgrade their courses according to market demand and teach practical skills relevant to the current job market.

Another issue that leads to emigration is the disparity in salaries. Professionals like doctors and teachers are highly paid in developed nations, whereas the same profession in underdeveloped countries receives a low income. This gap creates an incentive for individuals to seek job opportunities in countries like the US and Australia. To solve this problem, governments should take policy measures to revise salaries in underdeveloped countries to attract and retain skilled professionals.

Lastly, working hours may also contribute to the exodus of professionals. In poorer nations, employees typically work from 9 am to 5 pm daily. The strict working hours make it challenging for people to socialize or engage in personal activities outside of work. In contrast, developed countries like Europe offer more flexible work hours, with some employees only working six to seven hours a day. One potential solution to address this issue is for governments to grant special work hours to accommodate a better work-life balance.

In conclusion, migration of skilled professionals from poorer to developed countries results in a shortage of skilled labor in underdeveloped countries and stunted economic growth. Therefore, policymakers must create new employment schemes with better pay to encourage the retention of skilled professionals in underdeveloped nations.
