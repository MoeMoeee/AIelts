Revised:

As deforestation frequently occurs in many regions around the globe, it poses terrible threats to human survival. This essay will first discuss the primary causes of deforestation, including human activities and climate change. It will then explore the potential consequences, such as hotter climate and soil erosion.

To begin with, the primary reason for deforestation is human activities driven by various demands. It is widely acknowledged that in order to solve the shortage of accommodation, many regions are cutting down vast numbers of trees to clear land for construction or urban expansion. Moreover, large-scale deforestation for timber production is considered one of the most detrimental activities to the environment. Additionally, it is undeniable that climate change-induced wildfires can also significantly reduce forest cover. Recently, Australia experienced a two-year drought, leading to a severe bushfire season in 2019 and 2020, prompting the declaration of a state of emergency.

On the other hand, deforestation can have negative effects on climate and soil quality. Firstly, forests play a crucial role in regulating the air by converting carbon dioxide into oxygen through their leaves. If trees are indiscriminately cut down, the release of CO2 from factories and vehicles will increase, resulting in a rise in overall temperature. Consequently, deforestation contributes to global warming. Secondly, scientific evidence shows that when forests are cleared, soil erosion occurs at an alarming rate. Without the protection of tree cover, the soil becomes vulnerable to erosion by wind and water. This rapid erosion leads to poor soil quality, rendering it unsuitable for plant growth and potentially transforming the once productive land into wasteland.

In conclusion, deforestation is primarily driven by human activities, including land clearance for construction and timber production, as well as the occurrence of wildfires exacerbated by climate change. These events can result in global warming and soil degradation, posing a threat to various species, including humans. It is imperative that immediate and effective measures are taken to address these issues before irreversible damage occurs.
