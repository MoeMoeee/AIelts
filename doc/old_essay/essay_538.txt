Revised:

Some argue that governments should impose a ban on the sale of bottled water, claiming that water is a natural resource and should always be provided free of charge. However, I strongly disagree with this viewpoint.

To begin with, I believe that free access to water should only be provided in certain circumstances, such as public places, remote areas, and slum areas where people may struggle to obtain clean and drinkable water. For instance, in parts of India, clear tap water is easily accessible in villages, railway stations, parks, and bazaars. However, in urban areas where residential and commercial water usage is higher, it is reasonable for city authorities to charge monthly water bills as a means to discourage wasteful use. In fact, when tap water was free in urban areas some years ago, per capita water consumption was considerably higher.

Furthermore, banning the sale of bottled water is not a practical solution due to several reasons. Firstly, bottled water undergoes proper purification processes before being packaged, ensuring that it poses no harm to consumers. In countries like India, tap water can be heavily polluted and consuming it can lead to health issues. As a result, people often rely on bottled water for safe drinking purposes. Secondly, such a ban would face significant resistance from both companies and consumers. The bottled water industry generates substantial income, and any restrictions would negatively impact their earnings. Therefore, implementing such a ban is practically infeasible.

In conclusion, I strongly believe that governments should not impose restrictions on the sale of bottled water. Instead, providing free access to clean and drinkable tap water in public places and disadvantaged areas is a more viable solution. Additionally, considering the purification standards that bottled water companies adhere to, it is unreasonable to ban the sale of bottled water. Therefore, while ensuring easy access to safe drinking water is important, completely eliminating the sale of bottled water is neither logical nor feasible.
